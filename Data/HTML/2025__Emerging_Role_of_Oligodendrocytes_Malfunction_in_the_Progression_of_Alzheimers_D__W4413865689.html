Abstract

Alzheimers disease (AD), the most prevalent form of dementia, is characterized by hallmark pathologies such as amyloid-beta (A) plaque accumulation, tau hyperphosphorylation, and progressive neuronal dysfunction. While much attention has focused on neurons and microglia, recent studies underscore the significant yet understudied roles of oligodendrocytes (OL) and oligodendrocyte precursor cells (OPC) in AD pathology. OL, responsible for myelin production and maintenance, are impaired early in AD, contributing to demyelination, synaptic dysfunction, and cognitive decline. Emerging evidence reveals that A and tau pathology disrupt OPC differentiation and induce senescence, exacerbating neuroinflammation and reducing remyelination potential. Demyelination precedes overt AD symptoms, positioning it as a potential early biomarker. Furthermore, animal models reveal that OPC density and function deteriorate with age, particularly in the presence of A plaques, highlighting their vulnerability in the AD environment. Transcriptomic studies also link cholesterol biosynthesis and lipid metabolism dysregulation in OPC to AD progression, emphasizing the intricate relationship between OL, metabolic processes, and amyloid toxicity. Additionally, the identification of disease-associated oligodendrocytes (DAO), characterized by altered gene expression and proximity to A plaques, highlights their involvement in neuroinflammation and APP processing. This review synthesizes recent insights into OL and OPC dysfunction in AD, focusing on their roles in neuroinflammation, A clearance, and myelin integrity. It discusses the potential of targeting OL and OPC pathways, such as enhancing remyelination and mitigating senescence, as novel therapeutic strategies. By addressing gaps in our understanding of OL dynamics, this work sheds light on their critical contributions to AD pathology and sets the stage for future research and intervention.

Introduction

Alzheimer's disease (AD) has emerged as the most prevalent form of dementia worldwide, posing a substantial risk of cognitive decline, particularly in individuals over the age of 65 (Knopman et al. 2021). According to a 2022 report by the World Health Organization (WHO), over 55 million people are currently affected by dementia (World Health Organization 2022). Epidemiological projections estimate that the number of cases will rise to 113 million by 2050, positioning AD as a growing public health and economic challenge (Wu et al. 2017a). The distinctive pathological features of Alzheimer's disease, include the accumulation of insoluble amyloid-beta (A) peptides and the hyperphosphorylation of tau protein which, as AD progresses, lead to the formation of senile plaques (SP) and neurofibrillary tangles (NFT), respectively, and progressively disrupt neuronal function and connectivity (Zhang et al. 2024a; Abdelnour et al. 2022).

AD has a complex and insufficiently understood etiology involving a dynamic interplay between genetic predisposition, environmental influences, epigenetic modifications, and dysregulated molecular pathways (Talwar et al. 2016). Generally, AD is categorized into genetic (familial) and sporadic forms, with the latter being the predominant type observed in clinical populations. Genetic AD is a rarely occurring subtype resulting from mutations in APP, PSEN1, or PSEN2, which drive early-onset AD (EOAD) neurodegeneration typically manifesting before the age of 65 (Maurer et al. 1997). In contrast, sporadic AD, which comprises over 95% of cases, lacks a clear inheritance pattern and is primarily influenced by the APOE4 allele along with various environmental and epigenetic factor. EOAD, often associated with genetic mutations, exhibits a more aggressive disease course driven by increased A production, whereas late-onset AD (LOAD), is largely multifactorial, characterized by impaired A clearance, neuroinflammation, and oxidative stress (Strobel n.d.). Despite advances in understanding pathology onset, and progression, the multifactorial nature of Alzheimer's disease has hindered the development of a unified theory. Several hypotheses, including the cholinergic, amyloid cascade, neuroinflammatory, and oxidative stress have been proposed, each shedding light on distinct aspects of the diseases complexity (Liu et al. 2019).

The primary hallmark of AD-related neurodegeneration cognitive impairment stems from progressive neuronal cell loss and synaptic dysfunction. However, the role of glial cells in this pathology is insufficiently understood. Microglia, the main immune cells of the central nervous system (CNS), are perceived as a major contributor to neuroinflammation and oxidative stress in AD. While microglial activation initially serves to clear A aggregates through phagocytosis, prolonged activation leads to chronic neuroinflammation, further exacerbating neuronal damage (Singh 2022; Heneka et al. 2013). This is largely attributed to their harmful activation through the interaction with A and reactive oxygen species (ROS), which triggers proinflammatory cytokine (e.g., IL-1, TNF-, IL-6) and chemokine cascades (Bai et al. 2022; Leng 2021). This persistent inflammatory response disrupts synaptic plasticity, contributes to neuronal loss, and impairs cognitive functions. Additionally, microglial dysfunction in AD is associated with genetic risk factors such as mutations in the TREM2 gene, which encodes a receptor crucial for microglial phagocytic activity (Guerreiro et al. 2013). Loss-of-function mutations in TREM2 impair microglial clearance of A, leading to increased plaque burden and worsening disease pathology (Hou et al. 2022; Zheng et al. 2018).

Similar to microglia, astrocytes become reactive in response to Alzheimers disease pathology, a process known as astrogliosis. Reactive astrocytes undergo morphological changes, including hypertrophy and upregulation of glial fibrillary acidic protein (GFAP) (Kato et al. 1998). While these astrocytes can form a protective glial scar around A plaques to limit neurotoxicity, they may also contribute to neuroinflammation by releasing proinflammatory cytokines and ROS (Monterey et al. 2021). The interaction between microglia, astrocytes, and neurons is essential for maintaining CNS homeostasis. However, in AD, dysfunctional crosstalk with microglia leads to sustained neuroinflammation and oxidative stress (Hansen et al. 2018). Conversely, astrocytes can also interface with neurons to promote synaptic repair, regulate synaptic transmission, and preserve the structural and functional integrity of the bloodbrain barrier (BBB) (Zhang et al. 2024b).

Despite the undeniable role of glial cells in Alzheimers disease, the involvement of oligodendrocytes (OL) has remained relatively in a grey area. Like other glial cells, OL contribute to immunomodulation and the restoration of homeostasis during neuroinflammation (Theophanous et al. 2024). However, their most essential function lies in the production and maintenance of the myelin sheath, which insulates axons to enable rapid and efficient electrical signal transduction (Neely et al. 2022; Chen et al. 2022). The process of myelination continues throughout life and facilitated not only by mature oligodendrocytes but also by oligodendrocyte precursor cells (OPC), which proliferate and differentiate into functional oligodendrocytes (Maitre et al. 2023). Emerging evidence has confirmed that myelin breakdown and disruptions at various stages of oligodendrogenesis are significant contributors to the development and progression of AD. Patterns of impaired myelin formation correlate with the expansion of A and NFT during neurodegeneration (Braak and Braak 1996). Furthermore, demyelination appears to precede the onset of Alzheimers diseasesymptoms, positioning this process as a potential early marker of neurodegeneration (Desai et al. 2010, 2009; Stokin et al. 1979; Couttas et al. 2016).

This review presents the latest insights into amyloid- and tau protein-induced disturbances in oligodendrocyte precursor cells, oligodendrocytes, and myelin. It emphasizes the critical role of proper oligodendrogenesis and the profound implications of myelin breakdown in the context of Alzheimers disease. Additionally, it sheds light on the involvement of oligodendrocytes in neuroinflammation and the role of recently distinguished disease-associated oligodendrocytes (DAO), underscoring their significance in the pathophysiology of AD.

Oligodendrocyte Progenitor Cells (OPC) in Alzheimer's Disease

Oligodendrocytes represent 4575% of all glial cells, making them one of the most abundant glial cell type in the central nervous system (Han et al. 2022; Bartheld et al. 2016), originate from oligodendrocyte progenitor cells in oligodendrogenesis. This process occurs during both early postnatal development and adulthood. The production rate of oligodendrocytes declines with age, yet OPC remain abundant throughout adulthood, retaining the capacity for active proliferation and differentiation into mature oligodendrocytes within the CNS. In response to environmental or physiological demands, OPC, also referred to as N2 glia (NG2) or O2A cells (Nishiyama et al. 2009; Galichet et al. 2021; Duncan et al. 2018), differentiate into OL. This process is intricately regulated by a network of molecular factors, signaling pathways (Tiane et al. 2019; Sock and Wegner 2019), and complex transcriptional and epigenetic mechanisms (Tiane et al. 2019). These processes are essential not only for proper myelination but also for the dynamic remyelination necessary to maintain CNS function and repair (Akay et al. 2021). OPC play a pivotal role in a multitude of physiological processes, including autophagy, phagocytosis, and signal transduction via interactions with various cell types within the CNS. Their contribution to the maintenance and repair of the BBB highlights their multifaceted involvement in homeostasis and neuroprotection (Akay et al. 2021; Zou et al. 2023; Skaper 2019).

While OPC are primarily involved in maintaining the oligodendrocyte pool, a growing body of evidence indicates their involvement in the age-related changes and pathophysiology of Alzheimer's disease. In the A plaques environment, the production and accumulation of aggregates induces senescence in oligodendrocyte progenitor cells. This senescence shifts OPC towards proinflammatory phenotype, impairing their ability to differentiate and mature, thereby further exacerbating neurodegenerative processes (Zhang et al. 2019). Transcriptomic research on adult OPC has revealed age-dependent alterations associated with remyelination failure, including a reduced expression of proteins critical for maintaining stem cell functions (e.g., SOX2, SOX9, CDK1, CDK2 CHD7 and ARID1) and an upregulation of markers indicative of terminal differentiation states (e.g., PLP, MBP, MOBP, MOG, CLDN11, SNCA, APOE, and PADI2). Disruptions in cholesterol homeostasis have also been observed, with a decline in the expression of genes related to biosynthesis and phagocytosis. This results in a diminished capacity of adult OPC to clear and recycle cholesterol, ultimately contributing to decreased cholesterol availability in the brain (Fuente et al. 2020). Disturbed cholesterol biosynthesis and lipid metabolism are well-known AD hallmarks, and transcriptomic changes in adult OPC and the human hippocampus from AD patients overlap. Moreover, studies confirmed reduced gene expression of essential factors in cholesterol biosynthesis (e.g., DHCR24, HMGCR, HMGCS, ACAT2, IDI1, and MVD) (Fuente et al. 2020; Varma et al. 2021).

The animal models of AD have provided evidence of oligodendrocyte progenitor cell decline in density, and the following myelin integrity impairment in the hippocampus associated with age (Table1). Progressive changes in 3xTgAD mice were primarily observed in 6-month-old animals as morphological atrophy, which intensified over time, leading to OPC hypertrophy surrounded by A plaques (Vanzulli et al. 2020). Comparable disturbances in OPC have been documented in the APP/PS1 mouse model, with the earliest decline in progenitor cells number detected in 9-month-old animals. As the pathology advances, 14-month-old mice OPC exhibit pronounced morphological alterations, including cellular shrinkage, increased branching, and signs of dystrophy (Chacon-De-La-Rocha et al. 2020). These findings demonstrate that while OPC degeneration and morphological disruption are characteristic markers of aging, AD appears to significantly accelerate the onset and progression of these pathological processes.

OPC have emerged as pivotal players in the clearance of A, with their dysfunction has been linked to disruptions caused by A accumulation. NG2 glial cells have been identified as novel contributors to A clearance through mechanisms such as autophagy and endocytosis. While the role of OPC in A clearance is often considered less significant compared to microglial activity, research underscores that impairments in autophagic processes within these progenitor cells substantially contribute to the progression of AD (Li et al. 2013).

At the same time, a correlation between OPC presence and A levels in cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) from AD patients has been proposed as a critical factor in disease pathogenesis, highlighting the nuanced and multifaceted role of OPC in neurodegenerative processes (Nielsen et al. 2013). The clearance of A and cellular debris by oligodendrocyte precursor cells leads to an upregulation of clusterin production (Beiter et al. 2024), which could contribute to the worsening of AD pathology due to its role in mediating regulation of A-induced neurotoxicity (Foster et al. 2019). OPC from adult brain have also been identified as a cell type secreting A1-42, which is likely released in vivo in association with defective oligodendrocyte differentiation. According to recent data, this pathology may play a crucial role in the initiation and progression of sporadic AD (Nihonmatsu-Kikuchi et al. 2021). The restoration of the myelinating oligodendrocyte pool is also linked to morphological changes in OPC that occur in the presence of A plaques. In this context, OPC transition into senescent, proinflammatory cells that are unable to differentiate into mature oligodendrocytes. This conversion is regarded as a pivotal determinant of AD progression, as the ablation of senescent OPC in the APP/PS1 mouse model has been shown to significantly improve memory impairment (Zhang et al. 2019).

It is essential that future studies involving OPC and oligodendrocytes in Alzheimer's disease explicitly consider sex as a biological variable, given the pronounced differences in transcriptional profiles, functional responses, morphology, and physiology between male and female cells. Single-cell RNA sequencing of post-mortem prefrontal cortex from AD patients has revealed that female-derived OL and OPC exhibit a dampened gene expression response to pathology, which may underlie their greater vulnerability to white matter damage and cognitive decline (Kato 2025). In contrast, male OL show transcriptional activation correlated with pathological burden. Additionally, AD-associated oligodendroglial subpopulations are more abundant in females, while non-pathological populations are predominantly male. Beyond molecular differences, growing morphological evidence suggests that OL represent a heterogeneous population with distinct functional roles, such as varying preferences for axonal targets or differential sensitivity to myelination signals (Kamen et al. 2022; Osanai et al. 2022; Bechler et al. 2015). Recognizing the sex-specific diversity of these cells is essential to effectively target precision therapies for neurodegenerative diseases based on the sex of AD patients.

The Interplay Between Amyloid and Oligodendrocytes

Generally, the majority of evidence emphasizes neurons as the primary contributors to A production and distribution, thereby providing a central role in plaque formation associated with AD (Hampel et al. 2021; Murphy and LeVine 2010; Sadigh-Eteghad et al. 2015). However, recent research has slightly shifted its focus towards understanding the role of oligodendrocytes in this pathological process. The role of oligodendrocytes is currently considered complementary in A plaque formation, even though the expression levels of amyloidogenic -secretase 1 (BACE1) and amyloid precursor protein (APP) genes is comparable to those in neurons. Specific deletion of the Bace1 gene in mice reduced oligodendrocyte-derived harmful plaque formation by 30%. Thus, oligodendrocytes have the capacity to generate sufficient A to contribute to plaque accumulation (Sasmita et al. 2023, 2024). A similar observation has been identified in AD mice lacking the oligodendrocyte-specific Bace1 gene. These animals have shown a significantly diminished level of amyloid plaques in the hippocampus and cortex, with a reduction of insoluble A1-40 and A1-42 peptides (Ishii et al. 2024). Selectively suppressing oligodendrocyte-derived A production in AD animals prevents the worsening of neuroinflammation and cellular signaling disruptions that lead to neuronal hyperactivity, suggesting that the role of OL-derived A in Alzheimer's disease pathology is more likely focused on promoting neuronal pathological processes rather than generating A plaques (Rajani et al. 2024).

On the other hand, BACE1, the only known -secretase capable of initiating the production of A peptides, is crucial for the proper development of oligodendrocytes. Neuronal Bace1 deletion impairs the maturation of oligodendrocyte progenitor cells into myelinating oligodendrocytes, contributing to deficits in oligodendrocyte abundance and their capacity for axonal myelination (Hu et al. 2008; Benoit et al. 2023). These findings underline the need for caution in establishing therapeutic approaches for Alzheimer's disease mitigation due to the importance of BACE1 in oligodendrocyte development and function.

The ambivalence of oligodendrocytes and proteins related to myelination has been confirmed by numerous studies concerned with the interactions of A and leucine-rich repeat and immunoglobulin-like domain-containing protein 1 (LINGO-1) (Fig.1). This transmembrane protein, highly expressed in neurons and oligodendrocyte progenitor cells, plays a prominent role in the inhibition of oligodendrocyte differentiation and myelination in the CNS (Mi et al. 2005; Shao et al. 2017). As part of a receptor complex that signals to inhibit myelination, axonal regeneration, and synaptic plasticity, LINGO-1 binds to Nogo receptor (NgR) and p75 neurotrophin factor (p75NTR) (Mi et al. 2004, 2008; Zhou et al. 2012). Immunohistochemical and biochemical analyses performed using two AD mice models confirmed that administration of anti-LINGO-1 antibodies decreased A plaque deposition, enhanced OL maturation in the hippocampus, and improved myelin integrity in memory-associated brain regions, perceived in behavioral studies as partial restoration of spatial memory (Wu et al. 2018; Yang et al. 2022). LINGO-1 appears to facilitate A generation by enhancing -secretase cleavage of APP.(Fernandez-Enright and Andrews 2016) Diminished LINGO-1 expression had a positive impact on remyelination, the ultrastructure of myelinated axons, and elevated levels of myelin basic protein (MBP) (Wu et al. 2018), which is established as an inhibitor of A1-42 fibril formation by preventing the adoption of -sheet structures (Hoos et al. 2009).

On the contrary, LINGO-1 promotes, in a dose-dependent manner, the lysosomal degradation of APP, diverting it from the pathway that produces A (Fernandez-Enright and Andrews 2016; Laat et al. 2015). Nevertheless, NgR also emphasizes its negative influence as part of the receptor complex on A clearance. In APP/PS1 models, the overexpression of Nogo-A and NgR was observed to be age-dependent, contributing to synaptic dysfunction and neurodegeneration through the activation of the RhoA/ROCK signaling pathway (Guo et al. 2020). Elevated levels of NgR have been shown to impair the adhesion, migration, and phagocytic activity of microglial cells, thereby reducing their efficiency in clearing A (Wang et al. 2021).

Myelin Impairment in Alzheimer's Disease

The myelin sheath is a multi-layered, lipid-rich structure formed by the plasma membrane of mature oligodendrocytes. Wrapping the axons of nerve cells, the myelin sheath facilitates the rapid conduction of saltatory nerve impulses, thereby enhancing both the speed and efficiency of information transfer within the nervous system. Furthermore, proper development and maintenance of the myelin sheath is fundamental for axons protection, as they prevent ion leakage that could possibly disrupt the electrical potential essential for neuronal communication (Kondiles and Horner 2018; Stadelmann et al. 2019; Simons et al. 2024).

Alzheimers disease is commonly associated with grey matter disruption, however, a growing body of evidence from both animal models and AD patients reveals that white matter (WM) damage and consequently, a reduction in the myelin sheath are also correlated with neurodegeneration and cognitive dysfunctions (Filley 2021; Nasrabady et al. 2018). A clinically feasible modality to monitor demyelination and remyelination, called q-space myelin map MRI imaging, has shown that AD patients suffer reduced myelin density in, inter alia, the hippocampus, insula, and precuneus (Li et al. 2024). Patients with AD and dementia, with elevated levels of A1-42 in the CSF, exhibit an increase in white matter hyperintensity volume, which contributes to the deterioration of cognitive function (Li et al. 2024).

Desai et al. have conducted extensive research using 3xTg AD mice, showing various pathologies in the myelin sheath and oligodendrocytes (Table1). Abnormalities in 2-month-old 3xTg AD mices myelin sheath mostly manifested as disruptions in integrity, such as splitting of the major dense line and the accumulation of dense bodies in the Schaffer collateral pathway of the hippocampus (Desai et al. 2009). Importantly, the reduction in the CA1 hippocampus region and entorhinal cortex, as well as the decline in the function of oligodendrocytes, which were more prone to apoptosis, occurred prior to A plaques, even though OL degeneration was driven by soluble A oligomers (Desai et al. 2010, 2011). By 2months of age, APP/PS1 mice exhibited a thicker myelin sheath and a shorter internodal distance compared to wild-type mice (Wu et al. 2017b). By 12months, they also displayed a regionally restricted decline in oligodendrocyte density, specifically in the CA3 and CA4 regions of the hippocampus (DeFlitch et al. 2022). Myelin abnormalities were evidenteven at earlier stages in 5xTg AD mice. At 1month of age, transgenic mice exhibited a thinner myelin sheath, accompanied by splitting of the myelin layers and occasional ballooning, where the sheath became swollen (Gu et al. 2018). Moreover, an increase in abnormally myelinated axons was observed (Wu et al. 2017b). During the course of aging, myelin breakdown and OPC decline became apparent, with progressive deterioration in the hippocampus, particularly the dentate gyrus. By 12months of age, this deterioration expanded to encompass all regions of the hippocampus (Zota et al. 2024).

Prominent alterations observed in animal models of Alzheimers disease extend beyond myelin sheath pathology, encompassing dysregulation of proteins critical for the development and maintenance of myelin integrity. Myelin basic protein, the second most abundant protein in the CNS (Huang et al. 2024; Mller et al. 2013; Bauer et al. 2012), plays a crucial role in myelin sheath formation and maintenance, as it drives the assembly of myelin membranes through phase transitions mediated by hydrophobic interactions. MBP also establishes a barrier within the myelin sheath, expelling most soluble and membrane proteins to maintain the membranes structural integrity (Aggarwal et al. 2013). Furthermore, MBP has been identified as a potential inhibitor of A fibrils since it strongly binds fibrillogenic A forms (Hoos et al. 2009, 2007). A study by Tiwari et al. revealed interactions between myelin-like membranes and A1-40 (Tiwari et al. 2023). Amyloid peptide binds to the membrane, destabilizes its structure promoting A aggregation. Among various lipid compositions tested, L--phosphatidylinositol-4,5-bisphosphate (PIP2) was found to strongly facilitate A fibrillation during the early phase of aggregation (Tiwari et al. 2023). Importantly, PIP2 is a critical factor for MBP attachment to the plasma membrane in oligodendrocytes. However, elevated levels of PIP2 can induce excessive Ca2+ release, which destabilizes interactions with MBP and promotes demyelination (Nawaz et al. 2009; Kumashiro et al. 2021). Given this information, it can be presumed that MBPs capacity to inhibit A fibrillation depends on maintaining an equilibrium between PIP2 and MBP to preserve the structural integrity of the myelin membrane.

On the contrary, a study with 5xFAD MBP/ mice has shown reduced insoluble A and amyloid plaque deposition despite the lack of MBP (Ou-Yang and Nostrand 2013). Nevertheless, the study demonstrated that the absence of MBP led to increased expression of matrix metalloproteinase-9 (MMP-9), capable of degrading both soluble and fibrillar A. This triggered glial activation and increased A-degrading enzymes, reducing plaque deposition but potentially accelerating neurodegeneration through neuroinflammation (Ou-Yang and Nostrand 2013). Significant alterations in the levels and function of myelin basic protein have also been observed in other animal models of Alzheimers disease. In the 5xFAD AD model, the first signs of MBP decline were found in 1-month-old animals (Wu et al. 2017b). These results were confirmed in the APP/PS1 model, where by theage of 3months, animals began to manifest pathologies in the mRNA expression level of the Mbp gene (Dong et al. 2018). The worsening of this process was found in MBP hippocampus density in 9- and 14-month-old mice (Chacon-De-La-Rocha et al. 2020).

Although animal models of Alzheimers disease consistently demonstrate disturbances in the myelin sheath, the observed specific changes vary between models. Nevertheless, the presented abnormalities began to occur at an early stage of life, prior to cognitive impairment or even the classical hallmarks of AD. The loss of myelin due to oligodendrocyte death is significant, particularly as the myelin sheath generated during remyelination tends to be thinner and shorter compared to the myelin produced during development (Shiryaev et al. 2009; Franklin and Ffrench-Constant 2008).

Since preventing myelin loss remains unfeasible at present, improving the process of myelination is proposed as a potentially more advantageous strategy to improve cognitive outcomes (Wang et al. 2018). Genetically and pharmacologically enhancing myelin renewal could potentially improve performance in memory-related tasks. For instance, conditional deletion of the muscarinic M1 receptor (M1R) in OPC changed the density of newly formed myelin sheaths and enlarged the potential for repair. Improved myelin repair in these mice displayed significant improvement in the Morris water maze and novel object recognition (NOR) tasks (Chen et al. 2021). However, myelin renewal within the surroundings of A deposits seems to decline compared to areas lacking deposits, possibly due to inhibitory cues from senescent OPC induced by A deposits (Zhang et al. 2019).

This unforeseen issue alters the perspective on future research, suggesting the necessity to investigate not only myelin sheath changes in Alzheimers disease but also the underlying correlation between the hallmarks of AD, oligodendrocyte precursor cells, and mature oligodendrocytes.

APOE4 and Cholesterol Disturbances

APOE4, known as the second strongest risk factor for late-onset Alzheimer's disease (LOAD) after old age, is one of the three variants of the apolipoprotein E gene. Generally, ApoE protein functions as a major lipid and cholesterol transporter. However, the presence of the ApoE4 allele alters and impairs this function. APOE4 is associated with reduced lipid-carrying capacity, leading to poorer cholesterol metabolism and, consequently, cholesterol accumulation in the brain (Feringa and Kant 2021). It has long been known that disturbances in cholesterol metabolism affect microglia and astrocytes.

However, recent findings show that these disturbances also significantly impair oligodendrocyte function, leading to poor myelin maintenance and repair, which further contributes to neurodegeneration and cognitive decline in AD (Sharp et al. 2023). The impairment of the ApoE protein impacts oligodendrocyte and myelin function on multiple levels. In 5xFAD mice, the accumulation of apolipoprotein E within amyloid plaques coincided with the depletion of sulfatides in both white and grey matter (Kaya et al. 2020). Sulfatides, which are predominantly expressed in oligodendrocytes, are key lipids of the myelin sheath (Hirahara et al. 2017), where their abundance has been shown to correlate with the amount of myelin present in the rat brain (Norton and Poduslo 1973).

APOE4 carriers appear to have even more dysregulated cholesterol homeostasis. Blanchard et. al have revealed that these abnormalities occur in biosynthesis, transport, and storage. Furthermore, APOE4 presence results in intracellular cholesterol accumulation in lipid droplets while decreasing cholesterol availability for myelin production. Also, in post-mortem human brains, reduced expression of myelin-associated genes and decreased myelinated axons was observed (Blanchard et al. 2022). APOE4 impacts not only cholesterol metabolism but also directly influences the myelination process by altering oligodendrocytes (Kingwell 2023; Ahmed et al. 2024). The ApoE4 knock-in mouse model has demonstrated a decline in oligodendrocyte density despite no significant changes in myelin protein expression, indicating that oligodendrocyte loss precedes detectable myelin degeneration (Cheng et al. 2022). The detrimental effect on the density and number of myelinating oligodendrocytes is a multifaceted problem, as ApoE4 carriers exhibit an accumulation of oligodendrocyte progenitor cells, indicating an underlying issue with the oligodendrogenesis (Mok et al. 2023).

Importantly, alterations in oligodendrocyte morphology and function are closely linked to their interactions with astrocytes, which represent the primary source of APOE. Mok et al. not only demonstrated that the ApoE4 isoform disrupts oligodendrocyte function, but also elucidated that such impairments are directly associated with compromised lipid and cholesterol transport from astrocytes to oligodendrocytes (Mok et al. 2023). APOE plays a crucial role in maintaining neuronal homeostasis, primarily through astrocyte-derived cholesterol, and is also essential for preserving the resting phenotype of microglia and supporting their neuroprotective functions. Conversely, ApoE4 undermines astrocytic support for neurons, manifesting in mitochondrial dysfunction and impaired lipid metabolism in neurons, as well as the upregulation of proinflammatory genes in microglia (Preman et al. 2024; Li et al. 2021; Qi et al. 2021). Therefore, a comprehensive understanding of ApoE4's pathological impact on oligodendrocytes necessitates further investigation into the dynamic interplay between oligodendrocytes and astrocytes under both physiological and pathological conditions.

In neurodegenerative disorders including Alzheimers disease, the exacerbation of tau-related pathology is driven by the presence of ApoE4 isoform, facilitating its hyperphosphorylation and aggregation. The tauopathy mouse model P301S carrying the ApoE4 isoform exhibits various pathological hallmarks, namely: a larger area covered by hyperphosphorylated tau in the hippocampus, brain atrophy, thinner granule cell layers in the dentate gyrus (DG), and thinner layers of pyramidal cells in the CA1 region of the hippocampus. Importantly, the presence of this features was attenuated in animals with ApoE knockout, suggesting the significance of ApoE4 isoform worsening tau-related pathologies (Shi et al. 2017). While tau has been extensively studied in neurons, its role in other cell types is still very limited. Nevertheless, it has been proven that extracellular tau is internalized by microglia both in vitro and in vivo, and this process may contribute to cellular dysfunction, supporting the concept that tau secretion serves as a protective mechanism to limit intracellular toxicity associated with its pathological accumulation (Bols et al. 2016). Moreover, in astrocytes tau accumulation, known as aging-related tau astrogliopathy (ARTAG), characterized by thorn-shaped astrocytes, may represent an early response to neuronal injury, barrier disruption, or mechanical stress, and in some cases may precede neuronal tau pathology in neurodegenerative diseases (Kovacs et al. 2016; Kovacs 2018). Recent study by Torii et al. confirmed the presence of tau expression in mature, myelinating oligodendrocytes, which appears even later than classical myelination markers, making tau a potential new hallmark of cell maturity (Torii et al. 2023). Hence, tau role in initial myelin formation seems unlikely, however the fact that tau seeding was found in white matter points out alternative functions of tau in oligodendrocytes. It is speculated that tau in OL is involved in instrumental role in microtubule assembly, long-term maintenance and adaptive myelination (Torii et al. 2023; Majumder and Dutta 2024). Importantly, ApoE4 seems to at least partially influence faster tau accumulation and pathology mediated by myelin loss. ApoE4 carriers in tauopathy mice model manifested impaired lipid metabolism in all glial cells, including oligodendrocytes. The presence of ApoE4 isoform influenced cholesterol metabolism and promoted lipid accumulation in these cells (Litvinchuk et al. 2024). Reduced myelin fiber tracts were particularly vulnerable to tau accumulation, suggesting that areas with diminished myelin sheath density may be more susceptible to Alzheimer's disease (Rubinski et al. 2024). Single-nucleus RNA sequencing revealed that removing ApoE4 reduced phospho-tau coverage area and tau propagation. The absence of ApoE4 positively impacted not only tauopathy but also OPC activity and oligodendrocyte density. However, the direct correlation between these two hallmarks requires further investigation (Koutsodendris et al. 2023).

Role of Oligodendrocytes in Neuroinflammation

Microglia-Oligodendrocytes Interactions

Emerging evidence highlights the expanding role of oligodendrocytes, as active participants in the immune dynamics of the CNS. Their reciprocal interactions with microglia, the resident brain immune cells, significantly influence neuroinflammatory processes. Such interactions exacerbate neuronal and myelin damage, thereby contributing to the progression of AD pathology (Rossi and Bovenkamp 2024; Graham et al. 2025).

The understanding of microglia-oligodendrocyte interactions remains limited; however, existing evidence underscores the critical role of microglia in promoting remyelination (Fig.2). Microglia actively facilitate the recruitment and differentiation of OPC into mature oligodendrocytes through the secretion of key factors such as transforming growth factor beta 1 (TGF-1) and insulin-like growth factor 1 (IGF-1), which enhance the efficiency of remyelination (Baaklini et al. 2023). Both factors are significant in OLs maturation, although at different stages of development. TGF-1 is an important cell cycle regulator, and its signaling is crucial for oligodendrogenesis and postnatal myelination (Yu et al. 2000; Palazuelos et al. 2014). IGF-1, a neurotrophic factor responsible for A clearance and mitigation of AD pathologies, is indispensable for early myelin development (Arjunan et al. 2023). The lack of microglia-derived IGF-1 results in hypomyelination at the beginning of life and a thinner myelin sheath with ultrastructural deficits in adult life (Rusin et al. 2024). Notably, microglial support is pivotal for myelination specifically during adulthood, playing a crucial role in post-developmental myelin growth. At this stage, microglia prevent hypomyelination and influence myelin integrity through the TGF1-TGFR1 signaling axis (McNamara et al. 2023).

During the demyelination process, microglia contribute significantly to clearing myelin debris, which supports tissue remodeling and promotes conditions favorable for remyelination (Traiffort et al. 2020). This process, a defining feature of multiple sclerosis (MS), also observed in Alzheimers disease, leads to the accumulation of significant amounts of myelin debris in the central nervous system, predominantly composed of myelin-associated glycoproteins (MAG), well-known inhibitors of remyelination (Chen et al. 2023; McKerracher et al. 1994). During the initial stages of the clearance process, microglia remove myelin debris through TREM2-mediated phagocytosis, thereby attempting to restore homeostasis (Traiffort et al. 2020; Cignarella et al. 2020). However, as demyelination progresses, peripheral immune cells infiltrate the CNS by crossing the BBB and attack the myelin sheath. This infiltration exacerbates inflammation, activating both microglia and astrocytes, and accelerates the accumulation of myelin debris (Gao et al. 2024).

The accumulation of myelin debris directly impedes the proliferation and maturation of oligodendrocyte precursor cells into myelinating oligodendrocytes, leading to a failure in the remyelination process. This cascade of events perpetuates the cycle of neuroinflammation, demyelination, and impaired repair, further exacerbating CNS dysfunction (Prineas and Parratt 2021). In this proinflammatory environment, activated microglia become increasingly focused on clearing myelin debris, a process that diminishes their protective response to A plaques. This diversion of microglial activity compromises their ability to effectively corral and clear plaques, contributing to the pathogenesis of neurodegenerative diseases (Depp et al. 2021).

Astrocytes-Oligodendrocytes Interactions

Astrocytes are significant contributors to brain homeostasis as parenchymal cells, performing essential functions such as maintaining ion homeostasis, supporting BBB integrity, promoting neurogenesis and synaptogenesis, and interacting with oligodendrocytes to influence myelin regeneration. They also contribute to OPC proliferation, the development of mature oligodendrocytes and the modulation of neuroinflammation (Hu et al. 2023; Verkhratsky et al. 2023). The complexity of these interactions significantly impacts the development and progression of Alzheimer's disease.

Although astrocytes and oligodendrocytes originate from the same progenitor cells, they perform distinct functions once matured (Zuchero and Barres 2013). Nevertheless, astrocytes undeniably influence OPC proliferation, survival, and differentiation into myelinating cells (Fig.2). Astrocytes release a range of trophic factors, including platelet-derived growth factor (PDGF), fibroblast growth factor 2 (FGF-2), IGF-1, and TGF-, which are involved in the survival and development of OPCs into mature oligodendrocytes that are critical for efficient neuronal signal transmission (Rawji et al. 2020). Furthermore, astrocytes provide lactate-derived from glucose via monocarboxylate transporters (MCT), essential for axonal integrity and sustaining the high metabolic demands associated with myelin synthesis. However, astrocytes can also adopt inhibitory roles in remyelination. Reactive astrocytes can form glial scars that impede OPC migration and differentiation. These scars are characterized by the deposition of extracellular matrix components, which act as physical and biochemical barriers to successful remyelination (Lau et al. 2013).

One of the most prominent functions of astrocytes is maintaining BBB through tight junction stabilization, maintaining pH and ion homeostasis and preventing vascular permeability (Hu et al. 2023; Abbott et al. 2006). Oligodendrocyte precursor cells also contribute to vascular integrity by interacting with endothelial cells and pericytes. OPC secrete TGF-, which enhances BBB function under physiological conditions. However, in pathological conditions, OPC dysfunction can exacerbate vascular permeability (Niu et al. 2019; Seo et al. 2014). Also, in response to injury, including AD, astrocytes contribute to glial scar formation, which is critical in restoring BBB integrity (Sousa 2022). However, in demyelinating conditions, the glial scar also acts as a physical barrier that prevents OPC from accessing the demyelinated area and interacting with denuded neurons, thereby hindering remyelination (Wang et al. 2011; Nair et al. 2008; Silver and Miller 2004; Fawcett and Asher 1999).

Although astrocytes and oligodendrocytes impact neuroinflammatory processes, their functions seem to differ. Astrocytes and OPC produce proinflammatory cytokines during neuroinflammation, including TNF- and IL-1, which can have dual effects on the BBB integrity either supporting or impairing it (Bennett et al. 2010). These cytokines can also either protect or inhibit remyelination (Rawji et al. 2020). It should be emphasized that inflammatory action is initiated by microglia, which contribute to the activation of other glial cells (Singh 2022). While these cytokines can initially support OPC activation, chronic inflammation can suppress oligodendrocyte maturation and myelin repair (Liddelow and Barres 2017). Notably, excessive secretion of MMP-9 by OPC contributes to BBB breakdown, facilitating immune cell infiltration and worsening neurodegeneration (Seo et al. 2014).

A study by Sadick et al. shows shared transcriptional changes between astrocytes and oligodendrocytes in Alzheimer's Disease. The authors highlight overlapping responses of these cells in AD-related neuroinflammation, especially regarding NF-B signaling, interleukin-related inflammatory responses, oxidative stress pathways, and the downregulation of synaptic maintenance genes and genes involved in structural integrity, potentially contributing to widespread neurodegeneration (Sadick et al. 2022). Furthermore, pathological astrocytic activity seems to impair oligodendrocyte functions, especially remyelination during aging. Aging exacerbates the inhibitory effects of astrocytes on remyelination. Astrocytes exhibit increased reactivity in older individuals and secrete higher levels of chondroitin sulfate proteoglycans and other extracellular matrix components, further hindering OPC differentiation and remyelination. Additionally, aging astrocytes display a shift in their secretome, reducing the availability of neurotrophic factors necessary for OPC survival and function (Boisvert et al. 2018; Rawji et al. 2016). The interplay between astrocytes and oligodendrocytes is crucial for CNS homeostasis, myelination, and response to injury. While astrocytes provide essential trophic and metabolic support to oligodendrocytes, they can also contribute to remyelination failure through scar formation and chronic inflammation.

Disease-Associated Oligodendrocytes (DAO) in AD

The role of oligodendrocytes in neuroinflammatory processes extends beyond mere interactions with other glial cells, suggesting a more intricate and multifaceted involvement. Recent investigations utilizing single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) have drawn increasing attention to oligodendrocytes, consistently identifying a distinct population of disease-associated oligodendrocytes (DAO). These cells differ both functionally and morphologically from myelinating oligodendrocytes and actively participate in the neuroinflammatory processes characteristic of Alzheimers disease.

Transcriptomic alterations observed in 5xFAD mice underscore the influence of DAO on immune and metabolic dysregulation, as well as stress responses. Disease-associated cells demonstrated upregulation of Serpina3N, C4b, and Mhc-I genes, implicating them in neuroinflammatory processes (Table2) (Kenigsbuch et al. 2022). Interestingly, age-dependent overexpression of Serpina3N has been observed in ApoE4 mice within A plaques (Zattoni et al. 2022; Zhao et al. 2020; Licastro et al. 1999). Nevertheless, it is worth noting that this Serpina3N overexpression appears to have predictive value for Alzheimer's disease only in males, as the difference in expression between non-demented controls and AD males reached statistical significance exclusively in this group (Sanfilippo et al. 2025). Hence, such a sex-specific difference may indicate a substantially greater contribution of neuroinflammation to the development and progression of AD in males compared to females. In contrast, C4b is significantly associated with the protective effects of the ApoE2 genotype in Alzheimer's disease (Panitch et al. 2021) due to its proven ability to bind not only A1-42 peptides but also apoptotic and necrotic cells (Trouw et al. 2008).

Using scRNA-seq Pandey et al. confirmed the presence of DAO and classified them into three distinct subtypes namely DA1, DA2 and IFN(Pandey et al. 2022). Generally, the DA1 cluster was localized at the periphery of lesion sites during demyelination and contained genes overlapping with those identified in 5xFAD mice, and it was found to be associated with immune and inflammatory response pathways. However, as the brain progresses into repair phases, such as remyelination, these cells migrate toward lesion cores, indicating their dynamic involvement in both injury response and recovery. Notably, DA1 cells persist beyond the acute phase of inflammation, suggesting a potential role in chronic disease progression (Pandey et al. 2022). The presence of DAO-enriched regions found in demyelinated areas and disrupted axons in both multiple sclerosis and AD model mice implies that this cluster may reflect a broader response to CNS damage, rather than being specific to amyloidosis or tauopathy (Sadick et al. 2022). Interestingly, the DA2 cluster has the potential to provide a cytoprotective effect by countering the cellular stress induced by neurodegeneration, due to its association with cellular survival pathways, including PI3K/AKT and mTOR (Pandey et al. 2022).

In a study, Park et al. conducted exceptional research utilizing 5xFAD and App knock-in models to uncover a distinct gene expression profile for DAO, differentiating them from conventional myelin-associated oligodendrocytes. This DAO population, which exhibits an age-dependent increase, was characterized by the co-expression of Mbp and Cd74 (Table2). Notably, the upregulation of Cd74 was particularly striking, as this gene is not prominently expressed in homeostatic oligodendrocytes (Park et al. 2023). CD74 demonstrates a remarkable ability to bind the APP protein, thereby inhibiting the production of A peptides (Matsuda et al. 2009; Xu et al. 2024). Furthermore, the emergence of these disease-associated cells was consistently observed in close proximity to A plaques, where they displayed elevated expression of AD risk-related genes, including C4b and ApoE (Park et al. 2023), which the overexpression identified as DAO play a distinct role in the pathology of Alzheimer's disease, especially in the processing of APP. While alterations in gene expression are evident in both sexes, it is crucial to underscore the presence of pronounced sex-specific differences. Analyses of AD-associated genes in AppNLGF transgenic model demonstrated that Cd74 and C4b exhibit significantly higher expression levels in the cortical regions of female mice compared to their male counterparts (Castillo et al. 2017).

Due to limited research in the present field, the translational potential of DAO as a therapeutic target requires further investigation. SERPINA3N has been identified in the brains of post-mortem AD patients, and DAO has been observed in regions surrounding A plaques, suggesting potential avenues for therapeutic intervention aimed at mitigating DAO-mediated damage in AD (Kenigsbuch et al. 2022). However, transcriptional responses involving DAO in AD differ significantly between mice and humans (Pandey et al. 2022). Additionally, the transcriptomic signature of DAO overlaps with that of disease-associated astrocytes (DAA) and microglia (DAM), including the upregulation of a similar set of genes (e.g., C4b, Serpina3n, B2m) in response to amyloid and tau pathology. Despite these shared molecular features, glial cell types exhibit distinct functional profiles. For instance, astrocytes predominantly display interferon-related responses, whereas oligodendrocytes show changes associated with demyelination and apoptosis (Lee et al. 2021). Moreover, while DAM activation is heavily dependent on TREM2, DAO activation appears to occur independently from TREM2 signaling. This distinction underscores the possibility that oligodendrocytes may respond directly to amyloid or tau pathology without requiring microglial signaling pathways (Zhou et al. 2020). Understanding these shared yet distinct molecular pathways could inform the development of broader therapeutic strategies targeting glial cell dysfunction in neurodegenerative diseases. Nevertheless, the observed divergence between species-specific and cell-specific responses highlights the critical need to address the limitations of translating findings from animal models directly to human conditions.

Conclusions

The present review elaborates on the role of oligodendrocytes in developing AD-related pathologies. Presented evidence underscores the crucial yet insufficiently understood role of oligodendrocytes and oligodendrocyte precursor cells in AD pathology. While traditionally considered a neuronal disorder, AD also involves substantial glial dysfunction, with OL and OPC contributing to disease progression through impaired myelination, disrupted lipid metabolism, and neuroinflammatory responses. Myelin breakdown precedes cognitive decline, suggesting that OL dysfunction may be an early neurodegeneration biomarker.

A and tau pathology interfere with OPC differentiation and promote cellular senescence, exacerbating neuroinflammation and reducing remyelination potential. Animal models confirm age-dependent OPC decline, particularly in regions affected by A plaques, further supporting their vulnerability in AD. Transcriptomic studies link OL dysfunction to cholesterol metabolism disturbances, reinforcing the critical interplay between myelin integrity and amyloid toxicity. Additionally, identifying disease-associated oligodendrocytes underscores their active role in inflammatory pathways and APP processing.

Given the growing understanding of OL involvement in AD, targeting OL and OPC pathways presents a promising therapeutic avenue. Enhancing remyelination and mitigating OPC senescence may offer novel approaches to slow disease progression and improve cognitive outcomes. Further research is needed to elucidate the molecular mechanisms underlying OL dysfunction and develop targeted interventions to preserve myelin integrity in AD.
