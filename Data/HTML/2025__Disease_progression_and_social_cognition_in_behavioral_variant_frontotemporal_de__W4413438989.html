Disease progression and social cognition in behavioral variant frontotemporal dementia: Defining changes of the social brain

Jay Lydia Petronella Fieldhouse

    Research output: PhD Thesis › PhD-Thesis - Research and graduation internal

    105 Downloads (Pure)

    Abstract

    Summary of Findings The goal of this thesis was to gain insight in disease progression of behavioral variant frontotemporal dementia (bvFTD) and changes of the social brain. Greater understanding of the manifestation, course and relevance of clinical symptoms may help clinicians and researchers to adequately identify and monitor meaningful changes. Clinical manifestations: Part I addressed bvFTD heterogeneity. In a large cohort (N=315), age at diagnosis ranged from 26–85 years. Earlier onset was associated with more severe behavioral symptoms (euphoria, apathy, depression), while later onset was characterized by more prominent memory impairment. Survival did not differ across age groups, and nearly half were diagnosed after 65, showing late-onset bvFTD is more common than assumed. Music processing emerged as a relevant domain. In a prospective study (N=63), altered music appreciation occurred in 79% of FTD patients, most often musicophilia in bvFTD (35%). These changes were independent of general emotion recognition, though bvFTD patients performed worse on music emotion recognition. Music-related phenotypes may thus represent clinical markers and potential outcome measures. Disease course and clinically relevant change: Part II examined disease progression. A scoping review of 149 longitudinal studies highlighted established markers: FTD-specific rating scales (FTLD-CDR, FTD-FRS), informant questionnaires (FBI, CBI-R), cognitive screeners (ACE-R), and composites. Imaging markers included grey matter atrophy (MRI) and white matter pathology (DTI), while ASL and PET require further validation. Neurofilament light chain (NfL) emerged as the most promising fluid marker. A multimodal approach tailored to genetic and sporadic subtypes was recommended. Caregiver interviews (N=20) emphasized symptoms often underrepresented in quantitative assessments. Three core themes emerged: (I) loss of emotional connection, (II) preoccupation and restlessness, and (III) apathy and dependency. Impaired insight paradoxically reduced distress for patients but disrupted caregiver–patient relationships. Psychotic symptoms also impaired quality of life. Caregivers reported changing priorities across disease stages and highlighted major gaps in societal and medical support. The Social Brain Project provided longitudinal evidence differentiating bvFTD (N=63) from primary psychiatric disorders (PPD; N=26). While baseline socio-behavioral symptoms overlapped, bvFTD showed progressive worsening of behavior, emotion recognition, and socio-emotional sensitivity, in contrast to relative stability or improvement in PPD. Negative symptoms increased in bvFTD, while positive symptoms decreased in PPD. Higher serum NfL correlated with behavioral decline, underscoring its value as a progression marker. Socio-emotional changes: Part III employed multimodal approaches to socio-emotional dysfunction. In eye-tracking studies, bvFTD patients (N=18) showed reduced fixation on socially relevant facial areas (mouth region) and overall decreased attention compared to PPD and controls. In a comparative study of bvFTD (N=14), bvAD (N=12), typical AD (N=13), and controls (N=13), both bvFTD and bvAD displayed deficits in emotion recognition, empathy, and social norm knowledge. Shared features included reduced mouth fixation during emotion processing and informant-reported empathy deficits. Distinct patterns emerged: bvFTD showed rigid rule application and reduced biometric responses to personal moral dilemmas, while bvAD tended toward rule-breaking. This multidimensional assessment delineated overlapping yet stronger socio-emotional impairments in bvFTD. Conclusion: This thesis demonstrates that bvFTD is clinically and biologically heterogeneous, with age, phenotype, and caregiver context shaping symptom relevance. Progression is best captured by a multimodal approach combining behavioral, cognitive, imaging, and fluid biomarkers, with NfL as a leading candidate. Caregiver perspectives underscore socio-emotional symptoms as central to lived experience and emphasize the need for structural support. Finally, multimodal socio-emotional assessment highlights both shared and distinct features across bvFTD, bvAD, and PPD, offering opportunities for improved diagnostic differentiation. Collectively, these findings refine our understanding of bvFTD progression and the social brain, and support tailored, multidimensional strategies for diagnosis, monitoring, and care.
