Abstract

Introduction Visual perception is a fundamental skill that plays a central role in sensing the external environment. Deficits can lead to impairments in everyday activities. Visual perception is known to be impaired in Alzheimer’s disease (AD). However, the extent to which visual perception is impaired in mild cognitive impairment (MCI), often a prodromal state of AD, has not been sufficiently investigated. This study aims to assess visual perceptual abilities in people with MCI and mild AD compared with healthy controls. It will also investigate whether executive functions, activities of daily living and intelligence are affected in this context, providing new insights into the research of visual perception together with MCI and mild AD.

Methods and analysis People with MCI, mild AD and healthy controls will be assessed for cognitive deficits using the Syndrom-Kurztest (SKT). Visual perceptual performance will be assessed using the German version of the Developmental Test of Visual Perception-Adolescent and Adult: Second Edition (DTVP-A:2; Frostings Entwicklungstest der visuellen Wahrnehmung – Jugendliche und Erwachsene 2. Auflage). Activities of daily living will be assessed through the Erlangen Test of Activities of Daily Living in Persons with Mild Dementia or Mild Cognitive Impairment (ETAM) and the Bayer Activities of Daily Living Scale (B-ADL). Executive functions will be measured using the German adaptation of the Behaviour Rating Inventory of Executive Function - Adult (BRIEF-A) and intelligence will be assessed using the Matrix Reasoning and Vocabulary Test of the Wechsler Adult Intelligence Scale - Fourth Edition (WAIS-IV). Data analysis will involve descriptive analyses, linear regression analyses, multivariate analysis of variance, mediation analysis and structural equation modelling.

Ethics and dissemination This study has received approval from the Ethics Committee of Helmut Schmidt University/University of the Federal Armed Forces Hamburg, Germany (number: 2023_009). Findings will be disseminated through peer-reviewed publications and presentations at national and international conferences.

    STRENGTHS AND LIMITATIONS OF THIS STUDY

    • The study examines a comprehensive range of cognitive functions across different groups, thereby enabling a detailed and differentiated analysis.

    • The analysis will incorporate a range of data sources, including self-report, informant report and observational data, thereby ensuring a comprehensive evaluation.

    • The sufficiently large sample size within each group will enable the reliable identification of significant effects.

    • The study is dependent on pre-established clinical diagnoses of mild Alzheimer’s disease (AD) without the independent verification of imaging or laboratory diagnostics, which may introduce potential uncertainty regarding the accuracy of the mild AD group.

    • The assessment of intelligence may prove too complex for certain participants with mild AD, which could increase the risk of dropouts in this group.

    Introduction

    Mild cognitive impairment (MCI) is a prodromal state of dementia, characterised by a slow progression between normal age-related decline and dementia.1–3 MCI is characterised by marginal limitations in coping with complex everyday activities and slight deterioration in specific cognitive abilities (including memory, visuo-spatial skills, executive function (EF), language or attention).1 4 Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is characterised by a variety of deficits, including memory, language, EF and visuospatial abilities.5 Deficits in visuospatial abilities are mentioned in both MCI and AD.1 4 5 However, there is insufficient research specifically examining visual perception in MCI and AD. It has already been shown that visuomotor integration is impaired in people with MCI and worsens in people with AD.6 In the field of AD, studies have found evidence of impaired visual perception in AD,7 in addition to deficits in visual-spatial processing.8

    Current research indicates that visual problems not only occur in typical AD but are particularly pronounced in specific visual variants. For instance, in posterior cortical atrophy, a form of AD often termed as visuospatial AD, vision and spatial perception are primarily affected, while other cognitive functions are initially less impacted.9 Visual impairments also frequently play a significant role in other conditions, such as Lewy body dementia.10 Therefore, it is crucial to examine visual perception more closely and investigate its importance in the early detection of cognitive impairment.

    Individuals who will later develop dementia already exhibit impairments in various cognitive functions, such as memory, attention, visuo-spatial abilities, executive function or perceptual speed.4 Therefore, it is suggested that the diagnosis of MCI should always include both cognitive and functional abilities.4 Functional abilities are typically assessed via activities of daily living (ADL; everyday tasks), which include a wide range of everyday tasks such as preparing meals, managing finances, shopping or using transportation.11 Impairments in these areas can result in a loss of independence, personal responsibility and reduced participation in society.11 Several studies showed that impairments in ADL were already present in individuals with MCI2 4 12–14 and that cognitive impairments in ADL could be used as a criterion to discriminate between MCI and dementia.2 Individuals with MCI performed significantly worse than a healthy control group when coping with ADL.2 Additionally, cognitive performance is strongly correlated with ADL.2 11

    The objective of this study is to enhance the understanding of visual perception and its alterations in MCI, mild AD and healthy controls. Moreover, the study will investigate whether executive functions, ADL and intelligence are also affected in this context.

    Theoretical background and current state of research

    Mild cognitive impairment

    The prevalence of MCI is known to increase with age. In individuals aged 50–64, the prevalence of MCI was found to be 6.7%, while it increased to 25.2% in those aged 80–84.15 Research consistently indicated that individuals diagnosed with MCI have a higher likelihood of developing dementia compared with those who are cognitively unimpaired.15–20 Within 12 months, 10–20% of individuals with MCI developed dementia,4 and within 5 years, 72% of individuals with amnestic MCI developed AD.21 This is in contrast to a transition rate to AD of 1–2% in cognitively unimpaired healthy individuals.22

    The Winblad consensus criteria suggest the following criteria for the diagnosis of MCI4:

    • Abnormal cognitive decline that does not meet the diagnostic criteria for dementia due to the mildness of the symptoms.

    • There are no or only very mild impairments in coping with ADL.

    • Evidence of cognitive decline processes, for example by self-report or external report or by means of neuropsychological testing.

    While some studies did not consider impairments in ADL as a diagnostic criterion,4 23 an increasing number of studies suggested that ADL impairments already occur in MCI.2 4 12

    Different types of MCI can be distinguished based on the impaired cognitive domains.4 19 23 24 The most commonly mentioned type of MCI is amnestic MCI (aMCI), which involves mild memory impairment that is distinct from normal cognitive decline but does not meet the criteria for mild dementia.19 Non-amnestic MCI refers to cognitive deficits that exceed normal age-related decline processes and do not affect memory performance.19 25 These deficits typically include impairments in executive function, visual-spatial skills or language.19 25 This demonstrates the heterogeneous nature of MCI.11 26

    Mild cognitive impairment, activities of daily living and executive functions

    Several studies provided evidence of impairment of EF and ADL in MCI.11 13 14 However, not all people with MCI have deficits in EF; approximately 65% of studies found deficits in EF in people with MCI.27 It was shown that one in five people with aMCI have deficits in EF.11 Other studies showed that individuals with aMCI showed significant impairments in at least one EF component.28–30 A group comparison also revealed that people with aMCI and deficits in EF had significantly greater impairments in ADL than people without deficits in EF, although only a small effect size is reported.11 The results suggested that deficits in EF are an important factor in ADL functioning, as this relationship remains even after controlling for the level of cognitive impairment. A comparison of performance on EF tasks revealed significant group differences between healthy controls, people with MCI and people with mild AD.31 The results indicated that EF was influenced by cognitive impairment and that impairment of EF already occurred in MCI. Using regression analysis, EF was also shown to be a significant predictor of ADL in MCI.31 32 Furthermore, EF could predict ADL functioning even after controlling for cognitive factors.33

    Mild cognitive impairment and visual perception

    The relationship between MCI and visual perception has not been well studied. A study of healthy elderly people has shown that visual perceptual performance declines with age.34 There is also evidence that dementia disorders, including AD, are associated with deficits in visual perceptual performance.10 In relation to MCI, two studies examined visuo-motor integration as a subcomponent of visual perception.6 30 One study examined the differences between individuals of varying age groups and those with probable MCI.30 A decline in performance was observed with increasing age. In addition to the age-related decline in performance, individuals with MCI exhibited a further deterioration in visuomotor integration abilities as a consequence of their cognitive impairment. Another study compared a healthy control group, people with MCI and those with mild AD.6 It was demonstrated that there are notable discrepancies in visuomotor integration abilities between the three groups, with the latter exhibiting the most significant deficiencies. It could be concluded that visuomotor integration is a discriminating tool between individuals with MCI and those with mild AD.

    Mild cognitive impairment and intelligence

    A review of the literature revealed that approximately 71% of studies have identified significant changes in fluid intelligence in individuals with MCI.27 A study of people with MCI found low to moderate correlations between the level of cognitive impairment and intelligence.35 The largest effect sizes were found in the area of processing speed. Furthermore, there were slight differences in working memory performance between people with and without MCI.

    Functional relationships between visual perception, other cognitive factors and cognitive decline

    Visual information processing begins in the retina and is transmitted via the optic chiasm to either the lateral geniculate nucleus or the superior colliculus, both of which project to the primary visual cortex (V1) in the occipital lobe.36 From there, visual input is processed along two distinct pathways: the dorsal stream, projecting into parietal regions and involved in spatial awareness, motion perception and the control of actions; and the ventral stream, projecting via into temporal regions and supporting object, shape and colour recognition.37–39 The dorsal stream can be further subdivided into a dorsal-dorsal pathway, which proceeds from V1 through V2, V3, V3a and V6/V7 to the superior parietal lobe (SPL), and a dorsal-ventral pathway, which proceeds via V1, V2, V3, V3a and V5/MT to the inferior parietal lobe (IPL).40 41 Both sub-pathways ultimately project to premotor regions.39 The ventral processing pathway proceeds via V1, V4 and V8 to the inferior temporal gyrus (ITG).41

    In individuals with MCI and mild AD, altered activity in the terminal regions of these pathways—specifically the SPL, IPL and the ITG—has been consistently observed (see table 1). In MCI, functional activity in the SPL and IPL appears heterogeneous, with both increases and decreases reported,42 43 while in AD, a reduction in activity is typically found.44 The ITG often shows increased activity in AD, suggesting a compensatory mechanism.39

    Brain regions involved in visual information processing and the deficits in MCI and AD

    In addition to these pathways, visual perception involves both bottom-up and top-down mechanisms. One identified route runs from the occipital lobe via the intraparietal sulcus (IPS) to the inferior frontal gyrus (IFG), while another passes through the fusiform gyrus (FG) to the IFG.45 In MCI and AD, the IPS shows structural alterations, though the specificity of these changes to AD remains unclear.46

    The precuneus, which receives information directly from the occipital lobe, is implicated in higher-order cognitive functions such as executive processing and intelligence.41 47 48 It is hyperactive in MCI39 42 and hypoactive in AD.49 The precuneus also projects to regions such as the SPL, posterior cingulate cortex (PCC), primary motor cortex and FG.50 Notably, projections from the ITG reach not only the precuneus but also the medial frontal gyrus (MFG), anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) and PCC.51 These areas are also functionally and structurally altered in MCI and AD. The MFG is involved in visual perception, working memory and fluid intelligence tasks and shows mixed findings in MCI and reduced activation in AD.43 44 49 52 The ACC exhibits grey matter loss in MCI and a complete loss of functional connectivity in AD.53 54 Similarly, the PCC, which is linked to both visual perception and intelligence, displays reduced grey matter and activation in MCI and more severe deficits in AD.44 50 55 56

    The posteromedial cortex (PMC), comprising the precuneus, PCC and retrosplenial cortex, is a key region in visuomotor integration and visually guided motor control. Reduced activation in the PMC is significantly associated with a higher likelihood of conversion from MCI to AD.57 58 The posterior parietal cortex (PPC), as a multimodal integration area, plays a central role in combining visual and motor information and is linked to cognitive flexibility and intelligence.57 59 60 It projects to frontal regions involved in executive functioning.

    Taken together, the evidence suggests that in MCI and AD, functional impairments first become apparent in regions at the endpoints of the visual pathways,39 43 44 61 particularly those projecting into premotor and frontal areas.39 Visuomotor integration, as supported by these regions, may therefore be one of the earliest cognitive domains to show dysfunction in the progression of cognitive degeneration. Due to the overlapping roles of these brain areas in visual perception, executive functioning and intelligence, distinguishing disease-specific patterns remains a methodological challenge. Nonetheless, functional neuroimaging findings offer valuable insights into the interplay of visual and cognitive systems in neurodegeneration.

    Methods and analysis

    Aim of the planned work

    The aim of the present study is to assess the visual perceptual abilities of people with MCI and mild AD in comparison with healthy controls to examine whether there are any differences in visual perception between these groups. Moreover, the study will investigate whether executive functions and intelligence are also affected in this context. This is a pertinent issue in light of the identification of brain regions involved in both visual perception and executive functions. In this context, ADLs will also be investigated, as they are connected with executive functions and are known to be deteriorated in MCI and mild AD. Furthermore, with respect to intelligence, it is of interest to determine whether this acts as a protective factor, as higher intelligence is associated with higher connectivity, or whether intelligence changes as a result of degeneration in MCI and AD. The findings of this study will provide valuable insights for advancing research on MCI and mild AD, as the impact of visual perceptual performance in these disorders has not been sufficiently explored. The study offers a foundation for the advancement and refinement of theoretical frameworks by identifying the function of visual perception in healthy controls, MCI and mild AD. Furthermore, the findings can be used to develop methods for the early detection of potential MCI or mild AD, as well as to suggest preventive and therapeutic approaches.

    Research questions and hypotheses

    The hypotheses are formulated a priori on the basis of the literature review. First, the following research question will be investigated: To what extent do the degree of cognitive impairment and the constructs of visual perception, executive function and intelligence influence each other? The numbering of the hypotheses is in accordance with the preregistration. The hypotheses were sorted according to a thematic categorisation, resulting in a mixed numbering system. For purposes of clarification, individual words have been modified slightly in this hypothesis formulation, as originally preregistered; however, this does not entail any alterations to the content.

    H1: There are significant mean differences in the subtests of the Developmental Test of Visual Perception - Adolescent and Adult: Second Edition (DTVP-A:2) depending on the degree of cognitive impairment.

    H2: Visual perception functionality significantly influences scores on tasks of non-verbal intelligence and activities of daily living.

    H4: The level of deficits in executive functions particularly influences the results of the attention subtests of the Syndrom-Kurztest (SKT).

    H5: The level of deficits in executive functions and fluid intelligence mediate the relationship between the level of cognitive impairment and visual perception.

    Furthermore, the following research question will be investigated: How are the constructs of visual perception, executive function and intelligence related?

    H3: The level of deficits in executive functions and visual perception influence scores on a fluid intelligence task.

    In addition, a structural equation model will be constructed to answer the research question by examining the relationships between the latent constructs of EF, visual perceptual performance and intelligence.

    H6: Visual perception, executive function and intelligence are significantly associated as latent factors in a structural equation model.

    Furthermore, the research question of whether the level of deficits in EF and intelligence is associated with visual perceptual performance is investigated. This is formulated as an exploratory question due to the limited number of studies.

    Study design

    This study is an empirical, quantitative study with a cross-sectional design. The tests will be administered on two test dates to avoid fatigue effects. Between the data collection dates, the Behaviour Rating Inventory of Executive Function - Adult (BRIEF-A) and Bayer Activities of Daily Living Scale (B-ADL) questionnaires will be completed by the participant or a close relative not involved in the testing situation. There should be no more than 14 days between the data collection dates.

    Recruitment strategies

    Participants are recruited from the general population in Germany, in the north of Germany (Hamburg) and in the south of Germany (Erlangen/Nuremberg). Recruitment is partly done through a collaboration with the Memory Clinic of the Nuremberg Clinic for Psychiatry and Psychotherapy and partly through medical offices and personal social contacts.

    Influence of gender and/or diversity

    No gender effects are expected. It can also be assumed that a person’s social gender will have no effect on the data collected.

    Instruments for data collection

    Pre-testing/checking exclusion criteria

    Before the assessment begins, the participant is presented with a sentence to be read aloud to check their visual acuity. This ensures that the exclusion criterion of impaired vision is not present.

    Questionnaire for socio-demographic data and medical history

    Participants are given a questionnaire on socio-demographic data, including date of birth and gender, and questions on medical history, including problems with vision and hearing, diagnoses and therapies.

    Medical questionnaire (for participants with the group assignment to ‘mild AD’)

    Participants with the group classification ‘mild AD’ receive a medical checklist, which is completed by the attending physician. This questionnaire asks for information about the participant’s diagnosis and whether it has been confirmed by an appropriate diagnostic test. The physician is also asked to assess which description of the Global Deterioration Scale (GDS) levels 3 and 462 best applies to the patient.

    Assessment of the premorbid intelligence level

    Premorbid intelligence is crucial when current cognitive performance is impaired and no longer reflects a person’s actual abilities. An empirically and statistically developed scale with sufficient predictive power is used to determine a person’s premorbid intelligence level63 which is based on the Hamburg-Wechsler Intelligenztest für Erwachsene - Revision (HAWIE-R; German version of the Wechsler Adult Intelligence Scale, WAIS)64 and adjusted for the later version, the Wechsler-Intelligenztest für Erwachsene (WIE; German version of the Wechsler Adult Intelligence Scale - III, WAIS-III). Conversion guidelines are available, and correlations between the WIE and Wechsler Adult Intelligence Scale - Fourth Edition (WAIS-IV) allow further score adjustments.65

    Dementia

    The degree of cognitive impairment is assessed using the Syndrom-Kurztest (SKT).66 67 This screening tool measures attention and memory deficits and is suitable for the identification and early detection of MCI. It takes approximately 10–15 min to complete. Using cut-off scores, a participant’s performance can be translated into the severity of cognitive impairment.68 The internal consistency of the SKT has been demonstrated to vary in different studies, with Cronbach’s alpha reliability coefficients ranging from 0.80 to 0.88.68

    Based on a traffic-light coding method, the participants are assigned to the three groups ‘green=healthy control’, ‘yellow=MCI’ and ‘red=mild AD’ groups on the basis of the cut-off scores on the newly regression-based norms of the SKT.67 A score of 0–4 is assigned to the ‘healthy control' group. A score of 5–10 indicates the presence of ‘MCI’, and a score of 11–18 indicates the presence of ‘severe cognitive impairment probably due to dementia’. Whereas for the assessment of the cognitive decline, the newly normed SKT is used, the old norming66 is used only for the staging of the AD.

    For participants classified as yellow or red, medical diagnostics based on current classification systems can be carried out at the Nuremberg Clinic Centre, in addition to the SKT.67 The assignment of participants to these groups will be further verified using the Winblad consensus criteria4 and a medical questionnaire. To ensure the correct diagnosis of individuals with mild AD, a medical questionnaire is completed by the individual’s treating physician following the release of confidentiality. The questionnaire asks about the diagnosis and whether this was confirmed by imaging and/or cerebrospinal fluid examination, as these provide clear supporting indicators.69–71 In addition, the physician is asked to record the participant on the Global Deterioration Scale62 for assessment. Levels three and four are relevant, as they indicate the presence of MCI or mild AD.62

    Visual perception

    The Developmental Test of Visual Perception–Adolescent and Adult: Second Edition (DTVP-A:2)72 assesses visual perceptual performance. This is an internationally established procedure with a long history of development and tradition. The Developmental Test of Visual Perception, as devised by Marianne Frostig, has been in use for a long time and is regularly updated. In this study, the German version of the DTVP-A:2 named Frostings Entwicklungstest der visuellen Wahrnehmung – Jugendliche und Erwachsene 2. Auflage73 will be used. The DTVP-A:273 measures visual perceptual performance in individuals between the ages of 11;0 and 89;11 using the following seven subtests: Eye-Hand Coordination, Form Constancy, Copying, Visual Closure, Visual-Motor Search, Visual-Motor Speed and Figure-Ground. This subtest assesses fine motor coordination (Eye-Hand Coordination), perceptual constancy and flexibility (Form Constancy), visual-motor integration and spatial planning (Copying), visual completion (Visual Closure), visual search and attention under time pressure (Visual-Motor Search), speed and precision of visuo-motor responses (Visual-Motor Speed) and figure-ground discrimination in complex images (Figure-Ground). This allows four composites to be calculated. In addition to the ‘General Visual Perceptual Index’, a ‘Visual-Motor Integration Index’, a ‘Visual-Motor Efficiency Index’ and a ‘Motor-Reduced Visual Perception’ can be calculated. The test takes approximately 40 min to complete. The test procedure is expected to be published by Hogrefe in Göttingen, Germany, in Autumn 2025.

    The inclusion of both motor-reduced and motor-integrated subtests in the DTVP-A:272 enables a more comprehensive and ecologically valid assessment, distinguishing between perceptual and visuo-motor impairments.

    Executive functions

    EF are assessed using the German adaptation of the Behaviour Rating Inventory of Executive Function - Adult (BRIEF-A).74 75 The questionnaire, which is available as a self-report and informant-report form, using 75 items to measure the following nine scales to assess different aspects of EF: Inhibition, Self-Monitor, Plan/Organise, Shift, Initiate, Task Monitor, Emotional Control, Working Memory and Organisation of Materials. The scales form two broad indices, ‘Behavioural Regulation’ and ‘Metacognition’ as well as an overall summary score, named ‘Global Executive Composite’. The BRIEF-A takes approximately 10–15 min to complete. In this study, the self-report form and the informant-report form are used.

    Activities of daily living

    The Erlangen Test of Activities of Daily Living in Persons with Mild Dementia or Mild Cognitive Impairment (ETAM)76 is used to assess practical ADL in people with mild dementia or MCI. The test is designed as a performance test and is based on the International Classification of Functioning, Disability and Health (ICF).77 It measures the following five areas that have been identified as relevant to independent living with dementia: Communication, Self-care, Domestic Life and Economic Life.68 The ETAM consists of six everyday tasks: Making tea, Setting an alarm clock, Managing finances, Dosing medication, Making phone calls and Recognising traffic situations. It takes about 19 min to complete. The assessment is carried out by observing the test situations, in which evaluation and recording sheets are completed.76 The internal consistency of the ETAM in a sample of healthy individuals, individuals with mild and moderate AD was Cronbach’s alpha α=0.79.78

    Additionally, the Bayer Activities of Daily Living Scale (B-ADL)79 is used. The B-ADL is a screening tool for cognitive impairment in the sense of MCI and mild and moderate AD. The informant-rating instrument employs 25 items to assess the impairment of an individual in ADL by a relative. It takes approximately 5 min and includes questions regarding the ability to perform everyday activities and the presence of impairments in the ability to cope with specific demands of everyday life.80 The scale exhibits a high internal consistency with Cronbach’s alpha α=0.97–0.99.68

    Intelligence

    Two subtests of the German adaptation of the WAIS-IV81 are used to assess fluid and crystallised intelligence. The Matrix Reasoning subtest measures fluid intelligence. The participant is presented with an incomplete matrix and five possible answer alternatives, from which the correct one is selected. The Vocabulary subtest, on the other hand, measures crystallised intelligence, whereby the participant explains a series of words that are presented visually or verbally.81

    Data collection and setting

    Data are collected either in Erlangen/Nuremberg (Friedrich-Alexander University of Erlangen-Nuremberg or in Hamburg (Helmut Schmidt University/University of the Armed Forces and Medical School Hamburg). The tests are administered by trained test supervisors (i.e., master's students of psychology, PhD students of psychology) and take place on a face-to-face basis.

    Prior to data collection, participants are given the questionnaire, which is completed outside the test situation. The SKT is administered at the beginning of the first data collection session. If a participant achieves a score on the SKT that leads to exclusion from the study according to the exclusion criteria, the test will be cancelled. In addition, the DTVP-A:2 will be administered. Between data collection dates, participants will be asked to complete the BRIEF-A and the B-ADL. On the second assessment date, data are collected for the two subtests of the WAIS-IV and the ETAM. There is a maximum of 14 days between the test dates. The sequence of the tests is identical for all participants. Tests with higher cognitive demands are administered at the beginning of each assessment.

    Patient and public involvement statement

    Patients or the public were not involved in the design, conduct, reporting or dissemination plans of our research.

    Temporal scope of data collection

    The duration of the aforementioned test will last approximately 100 min, split into two dates. An additional 30 min is required for the participant to complete the sociodemographic questionnaire and the BRIEF-A. For relatives, the BRIEF-A and the B-ADL in the external assessment version will also take approximately 30 min to complete. These questionnaires can be completed at any time outside the test situation.

    Pilot testing

    The feasibility and duration of the planned study were analysed in a pilot testing. The results confirmed the feasibility of the study with the group of participants to be analysed.

    Statistical analysis

    Sample size calculation

    A total sample size of n=188 participants is targeted.

    An a priori power analysis was performed using G*Power 3.182 to determine the optimal sample size. The key numbers for the input are α=0.05, power 1-β = 0.90, effect size d=0.5 or f 2 = 0.15, f=0.25. Wherever possible, two-tailed tests are used.

    • Multiple linear regression with seven predictors results in an optimal sample size of n=55 participants.

    • Multivariate analysis of variance (MANOVA) with three groups results in a sample size of n=130 participants or n=44 per group.

    • Structural equation modelling requires a sample size of at least n=100 participants.

    Data analysis

    The data will be analysed using SPSS 27,83 starting with descriptive statistics. The hypotheses formulated a priori will be tested. For this purpose, correlation analyses, multiple linear regression models, MANOVA and post hoc tests, mediation analyses and a structural equation model will be calculated. For the latter, the SPSS extension AMOS is used.

    Overview of the statistical evaluation methods for each hypothesis. For purposes of clarification, individual words have been modified slightly in this hypothesis formulation, as originally preregistered; however, this does not entail any alterations to the content:

    • H1: There are significant mean differences in the subtests of the DTVP-A:2 depending on the degree of cognitive impairment.

      • Evaluation: MANOVA and post hoc tests

    • H2: Visual perception functionality significantly influences scores on tasks of non-verbal intelligence and activities of daily living.

      • Evaluation: multiple linear regression

    • H3: The level of deficits in executive functions and visual perception influences scores on a fluid intelligence task.

      • Evaluation: multiple linear regression

    • H4: The level of deficits in executive functions particularly influences the results of the attention subtests of the SKT.

      • Evaluation: multiple linear regression

    • H5: The level of deficits in executive functions and fluid intelligence mediates the relationship between the level of cognitive impairment and visual perception.

      • Evaluation: mediation analysis

      • Evaluation: structural equation modelling

    Dealing with missing data

    If incomplete datasets occur, the analyses will be based on pairwise deletion.

    Data management and legal aspects

    Informed consent

    Participation in the study is only possible with prior written consent after being informed about the study. All individuals must be capable of giving consent. Therefore, people with legal guardians will not be recruited. Participation is voluntary; non-participation or withdrawal from the study will not result in any disadvantages or negative consequences for the participant. Withdrawal of consent is possible as long as personal data are in the database.

    Anonymisation

    The data collection will be pseudonymised in order to assign data points. Participants are randomly assigned an individual code, which they enter during data collection and on the questionnaires. Once all data collection has been completed, the data will be anonymous. The data will only be analysed and processed in an anonymous form. The study will be conducted in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki, good scientific practice and national legislation.

    Summary

    The present study is aimed at improving the understanding of MCI by investigating the impairment of visual perceptual performance and the influence of ADL, EF and intelligence in MCI. MCI is characterised by marginal impairment in complex daily activities and mild deterioration of specific cognitive abilities, occurring between the normal age-related decline and the development of dementia.2 It is probable that individuals with MCI will develop dementia over time, particularly AD.4 19 84 The results of this study will provide important information for research into MCI, as the influence of visual perceptual performance in this disorder has not been sufficiently investigated.

     A major strength of this study is that several constructs are examined in order to obtain the most comprehensive overview of the differences in abilities or cognitive domains between the healthy control group, MCI and AD. This is particularly important given that MCI is a very heterogeneous condition.11 26 Although our research group combines expertise from psychology, medical psychology and psychiatry, we rely on treating physicians to confirm diagnoses of mild AD, as we do not conduct laboratory and imaging diagnostics ourselves. Consequently, we are reliant on the confirmation of the diagnosis by the treating physicians. To address this, we require participants to have clinical confirmation of mild AD via a medical questionnaire.

    Study status

    Participant recruitment has begun at the end of 2023. Currently, participant recruitment of patients with mild AD is taking place beside the recruitment of the healthy control group. Data collection will continue until the beginning of 2026.

    Ethics and dissemination

    A data protection and ethical approval has already been carried out by the Helmut Schmidt University as part of the standardisation and validation study of the German version of the DTVP-A:2. An additional application for this planned project has been approved by the ethics committee of the Helmut Schmidt University/University of the Federal Armed Forces Hamburg, Hamburg, Germany (number: 2023_009). The study will be performed in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki, Good Clinical Practice principles and relevant regulations. Participation is voluntary, and a written informed consent is obtained from all participants. The socio-demographic questionnaire collects personal data (date of birth, gender, education) and two special categories of personal data (ethnic origin, health data). The principle of data minimisation is respected. All data to be collected is specifically related to the purpose of the processing and is necessary to fulfil that purpose.

    The results of the study will only be published in peer-reviewed scientific journals in a fully anonymised form. In addition, the data will be used to write scientific qualification papers for Bachelors, Masters and Doctoral degrees. The Memory Clinic of the Nuremberg Clinic for Psychiatry and Psychotherapy will be involved in the design of the scientific articles and the recruitment of at least 10 participants. The clinic is also involved in the proofreading of the manuscript and authorship.
